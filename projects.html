Aircraft routing and scheduling for Mumbai International Airport

Master's thesis ( Prof. Ranade, Prof. Pant)

Due to steep increase in air-transportation demand, heavy air traffic has become very common at many airports across the world while airport facilities and operation methodologies broadly remain constant. The throughput of an airport in terms of the the number of aircraft handled, depends on the airport's infrastructural capacity and operational efficiency. Due to heavy increase in air transportation, many of the airports today work at the brink of their infrastructural capacity thus making efficient operations a significant determining factor. In this project, we are working in collaboration with the Mumbai International Airport Limited (MIAL) to develop a decision support tool for Air Traffic Controllers (ATCs) to assist them in making better decisions for optimal routing and scheduling of aircraft through the network of runways and taxi-ways.

Optimal aircraft scheduling is a well known NP-Hard problem and hence heuristics are often used to generate schedules within realistic run-times. These heuristics are designed to run fast, but often do not promise any guarantee about the solution quality. A metric to evaluate and benchmark various scheduling algorithms for their nearness to optimality is thus necessary. For the first part of the project, I have developed an exhaustive search algorithm that finds a provably best solution to a snapshot aircraft scheduling problem. The airport map (interpreted as a graph) and details of aircraft within the scheduling horizon are taken as inputs and the globally optimal schedule that minimizes the total taxi and waiting time for all aircraft is generated. This has been done by determining all simple-paths for the aircraft and identifying the global optimum via an MILP (using CPLEX) run on every flight-path combination.

From this work we observed that due to the limited size of the airport, merely arranging the flight-path allocations in increasing order of path lengths leads to the global optimum being reached at a very early stage of the run. For the second stage of the project, we have developed a Branch & Bound algorithm to cut down on the run-time of the previous approach. With this approach, we are able to identify the lower and upper bounds for schedules of every flight-path combination. The overlaps are modelled as disjunctive constraints and the BnB tree for every such combination is thus grown and pruned to finally identify the optimal schedule for that particular combination. The final optimal schedule is a minimum over all such flight-path combinations and is therefore a global optimum. Dramatic improvements over the previous brute-force approach have been observed using this algorithm. The two test problems from our AIAA paper now take merely 14 and 15 seconds repectively. The final objective of the project is to come up with a method to find a testable optimal schedule for aircraft on an airport on the fly, subject to variability of parameters in real-time.

Parallel framework for PySPH

Bachelor's thesis (Prof. Ramachandran, Prof. Chatterjee)

Publications:

P. Godbole, K. Puri, P. Ramachandran, "Load-balancing strategies for parallel Smooth Particle Hydrodynamics"
8th International SPHERIC workshop 2013 - Trondheim, Norway (Presenter)
(Presentation), (Paper)

K. Puri, P. Godbole, P. Ramachandran, "Dynamic Load-balancing for particle methods"
PARTICLES 2013 - Stuttgart, Germany
(Presentation)

K. Puri, P. Ramachandran, P. Pandey, C. Kaushik, P. Godbole, "PySPH: A Python framework for Smooth Particle Hydrodynamics"
8th International SPHERIC workshop 2013 - Trondheim, Norway
(Paper)

PySPH is an open source framework for Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) simulations being developed under Prof. Prabhu Ramachandran, Aerospace Engineering Department, IIT-Bombay over the last 5 years. I was introduced to PySPH in my third year by Prof. Ramachandran. In my fourth year, I carried over this work to my bachelor's thesis, where I worked under the mentorship of Kunal Puri (a PhD student) on developing a new parallel module for PySPH.

In the parallel implementation of Lagrangian (coordinates move with the fluid) methods like SPH, the simulation space is geometrically divided into sub-problems across processors which then solve the problem colaterally. But SPH being a particle method, it is impossible to sub-divide the simulation into mutually independent sub-problems and hence boundary data needs to be exchanged across neighboring processors at every iteration for the simulation to give accurate results. Thus efficient load-balancing (appropriate distribution of data across processors that ensures equal work load and minimal data-exchange) and message passing becomes very critical.

The final opbective was to implement an application agnostic parallelizer for PySPH. We used Zoltan for the various geometric and graph based load-balancing algorithms. I contributed to the integration of Zoltan with PySPH and evaluation of all the load-balancing methods. I presented this work at the SPHERIC 2013 conference in Trohdheim, Norway. An extension of the same to general particle methods was then presented at PARTICLES 2013 conference in Stuttgart, Germany by Kunal. I also proposed a new strategy for neighbor particle search based on modified symetric-interactions which achieved a speed-up of 25%. The parallelizer we developed achieved a near-linear scale-up for upto 128 processors with speeds comparable to other established packages.

Multi-track Railway scheduling using MILP

Summer Project (Prof. Ranade)

On prof. Ranade's suggestion, I participated in the INFORMS RAS 2012 competition and worked on it's problem statement as a summer project. Although I did not win the contest, I was able to deliver a working railway scheduler with significant realism. This project whetted my interest in Linear programming and led me to continue working under Prof. Ranade for my master's thesis.

The railway scheduling algorithm takes as input the multi-track railway territory map (interpreted as a graph) and the parameters of all trains and gives as output the nodal-time-stamps for every train such that the given cost function is minimized. It takes into account various realistic constraints and conditions such as terminal want times, intermediate arrival times, on-track waiting times, unpreferred track usages, priority allocations, maintainance windows, train types, lengths, speeds and weights while evaluating the cost function. In short, it works in two steps. In the first step, trains are greedily allocated paths based on the train priority, starting time and other cost considerations. In the second step, an optimal schedule to minimize the total cost is determined via an MILP model (using CPLEX). To improve the schedule, some path allocations are randomly swapped and the cost function re-evaluated to check improvement over the base schedule. The entire algorithm is nested inside a loop which splits the trains into batches and schedules one batch at a time to ensure scalability w.r.t. the number of trains as the problem size increases.

Pratham Ground station

Pratham (IIT-Bombay student satellite)

I joined the Pratham team in August 2010, in my sophomore year, as member of the Communications sub-system. Pratham's payload is to measure the TEC (Total Electron Count) of the atmosphere over India. We have collaborated with 11 other universities in India and with University of Paris - Diderot (IPGP), in Paris to set up IIT-Bombay's sister ground-stations in their own campuses. With the help of these ground-stations, we endeavor to generate an electron-density map in regions with multiple ground-station visibility.

In the Communications sub-system, I was responsible for developing the downlink data-decoding and data-management system for Pratham. As part of the ground-station team, I contributed to the setup and automation of the IIT-Bombay ground-station. With the ground-station, we tracked and decoded over 20 satellites including the International Space Station. I also got to visit other universities to assist them in replicating the IIT-Bombay ground-station. The data-decoding system was developed here at IIT-Bombay while the design of the data-management system was done during my internship at IPGP.

Additionally, I suggested a slight redesign and reprogramming of the beacon circuit on-board Pratham, which reduced the beacon power consumption by 50%. The solution is quite simple and worth mentioning. In short, it goes as follows:
The beacon signal emitted by Pratham is OOK (On-Off Keying) encoded and has a duty-cycle of 50% to maintain the power budget. The signal generation is achieved using an oscillator on the beacon circuit. Initially the beacon message, encoded in morse-code was input to the enable pin of the oscillator while the power pin was always on. Thus the oscillator consumed some power even when it was not transmitting. And the 50% duty cycle meant that the beacon signal could be transmitted only for 50% of the time, leading to data loss. I suggested that since we were using OOK, we could actually power off the oscillator entirely for the part of the message when '0' was being transmitted and power it on while transmitting a '1', essentially changing the input of the beacon message from enable pin to power pin of the oscillator. The response time of the oscillator was found to be short enough for this hack to work well. The message was then appropriately modified so that the on-time (number of spaces) was approximately equal to the off-time (number of 'dits' + 3 x 'das') in the morse code being transmitted, ensuring the 50% duty cycle. So we were able to achieve constant transmission of beacon signal while satisfying our power budget.

Multi-target Particle Swarm Optimization

Supervised Learning Project (Prof. Joshi)

Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) is a nature-inspired heuristic method to optimize the objective function via a population of candidate solutions, dubbed boids, exploring the solution-space based on simple mathematical rules for their position, velocity and cost function. The algorithm is inspired from the behaviour of birds in search of food. Similar to the birds, the boids have both exploratory and swarming tendencies, appropriately balancing which leads to convergence to an optimum in the solution-space.

For this project I implemented a PSO algorithm to work on solution spaces with multiple optima and evaluated and analysed the effects of the driving parameters (acceleration coefficients) on speed and efficiency of the PSO. We made critical observations and quantified the performance of the algorithm based on values of these coefficients. After qualitatively reasoning the observations, we proposed two approaches to improve the multi-target PSO.

Remote I/O interface for FlightGear

Supervised Learning Project (Prof. Ramachandran)

FlightGear is an open-source flight simulator with a goal to create a sophisticated and open flight simulator framework for use in research or academic environments, pilot training and as an industry engineering tool.

FlightGear has an elaborate backend data interface with variety of pre-defined and generic protocols. The objective of this project was to develop a remote Input/Output interface for FlightGear that could be used to track and evaluate the performance of a flier or a control law. I developed a generic UDP socket protocol and a GUI interface to remotely set and get the flight parameters of FlightGear. The GUI was also capable of dynamically plotting various flight parameters in real-time thus enabling the flier to evaluate his performance. The implementation of the interface was done in Python.

The project led me to develop a very basic terminal based ip chat application that allowed me to chat with institute friends even when the internet was down.
